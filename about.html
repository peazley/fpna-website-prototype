About FPNA

A neighborhood association at the edge of one of America's largest urban forests.

FPNA is one of the City of Portland's 94 neighborhood associations. We represent residents from W. Burnside north to Cornelius Pass and Newberry Roads, and west to the Washington County line — a unique stretch of city that runs through both incorporated Portland and unincorporated Multnomah County.

Mission

FPNA provides a forum for neighbors to discuss diverse issues — from traffic to wildlife, land-use plans to noxious weed removal, park protection to fire safety. We advocate for effective governance, safety, livability, and protection of the rural and wild nature of the neighborhood.

We are part of a wildlife corridor that connects into Oregon's Coast Range, supporting the biodiversity and wildness of Forest Park. A neighborhood survey showed our residents overwhelmingly value our regionally important wildlife habitat and headwater streams. We have worked to protect the valuable natural resources in and around Forest Park since our founding in 1976.

The Portland metropolitan area is growing, which means increased pressure for development as well as all the factors that accompany a burgeoning population. FPNA automatically receives land use notices about development within its boundaries and can appeal decisions at the city level without charge.

Some of our past accomplishments

  • Urban and Rural Reserves process — helping to determine areas suitable for growth and areas needed to maintain viability of agriculture and forest industries or protect natural landscape features
  • Forest Park Mountain Biking Trail Committee — advocating for Forest Park's natural resources
  • Cornell Road Sustainability Coalition — addressing Cornell Road traffic issues
  • Annual SOLVE clean-up — cleaning up road debris on neighborhood roads and in the park
  • Eradication of noxious weeds — garlic mustard, ivy, holly
  • Winter and summer gatherings for neighborhood residents

What we are not

FPNA is not a homeowners' association. Membership is voluntary, there are no dues, no covenants, no design review, and no authority over private property. We are an organized voice for residents, not a regulatory body.

Boundary

Where the neighborhood begins and ends.

The FPNA boundary follows the residential edge of Forest Park itself, plus the surrounding ridge and valley communities. Many residents are in unincorporated Multnomah County; others are within the City of Portland. The boundary is erratic in places — a reflection of how this corner of the city actually developed.

A short history of advocacy

Forest Park, the neighborhood, and the work that has shaped both.

FPNA is fifty years old. The park it surrounds is older still — and the work of stewarding it has been generational. A few of the milestones that brought us here.

1897
Macleay donation
Scottish-born Portland businessman Donald Macleay donates approximately 105 acres of forested land to the City of Portland — the foundational acreage for what would eventually become Forest Park.
1903
Olmsted Brothers Portland Park Plan
John Charles Olmsted and Frederick Law Olmsted Jr. propose that the densely wooded hills west of downtown Portland be preserved as a forest park. The plan is the origin of the name.
1943
Robert Moses postwar plan
The Portland Area Postwar Development Committee, including shipping magnate Edgar Kaiser, commissions Robert Moses to plan postwar Portland. The plan reaffirms the case for a Forest Park.
1948
Forest Park dedicated
After nearly half a century of advocacy, the City of Portland formally dedicates Forest Park on September 25, 1948 — as an "unimproved" park, without playgrounds or picnic areas. The decision sets the tone for community stewardship.
1948+
Forest Park grows
Through subsequent decades, the city acquires additional land, including much of Balch Creek. Forest Park today is over 5,000 acres — one of the largest urban forests in the United States.
1976
FPNA founded
A group of residents organizes formally to represent the neighborhood around Forest Park in conversations with the City of Portland and Multnomah County.
1994–1995
Forest Park Natural Resources Management Plan
FPNA serves on the Citizens Advisory Committee that develops the City of Portland's governing plan for the park's protection and management. The NRMP remains the substantive standard for decisions affecting Forest Park today.
2001
25th anniversary
The association marks 25 years with a community gathering and the first published history of the neighborhood.
2007
Oregon Senate Bill 1011: Reserves framework
SB 1011 authorizes Metro and surrounding counties to designate Urban and Rural Reserves — a long-horizon framework for shaping where Portland's growth boundary can and cannot move. FPNA participates in the process.
2010–2011
Grand Bargain finalized
Following years of negotiation under SB 1011, Metro and Multnomah, Washington, and Clackamas counties reach the Reserves Grand Bargain — substantive protection for the perimeter of Forest Park.
2016
40th anniversary & partnership expansion
FPNA deepens its partnerships with Forest Park Conservancy, Skyline Ridge Neighbors, and other regional organizations.
2019
Metro Parks and Nature Bond
Voters approve a $475 million bond authorizing land acquisition, restoration, and access investments across the region.
2024–2025
Coalition transition: NWNW to D4C
FPNA moves from Neighbors West-Northwest to the new District Four Coalition as part of the City of Portland's transition to a district-based council structure.
2025
PGE Harborton Phase 3 overturned
A coalition including FPNA, Forest Park Conservancy, and Bird Alliance of Oregon successfully overturns a city decision permitting PGE to clear-cut mature forest for transmission expansion. See Land Use for details.
2026
Fifty years
FPNA marks half a century of community advocacy. Anniversary celebration Saturday, August 22, 2026, 2:00–5:00 PM at Skyline Tavern.
The board

The volunteers who keep the association running.

FPNA directors are elected by the membership. Terms are three years, staggered so approximately one-third of seats are up each year. Officers (President, Vice President, Secretary, Treasurer) are elected by the board from among the directors. Per bylaws Article VIII §3 and §6.
